Brief Summary of the Book

The book follows the main character’s, Njoroge, upbringing from a young boy till a young adult in the twilight of the colonial period in Kenya. Njoroge is used, in a way, as a vehicle to describe the effects the political turmoil of the struggle for independence and the mau mau rebellion in the country, then British colony, had on the people of Kenya. And the emergent outlooks people held on the path to national independence. The two paths of either armed struggle or reconciliation and peaceful dialogue with the oppressor. Njoroge initially places high value on education as a way out of poverty and a way to develop the country, but as he grows older he becomes disillusioned the the omnipotence of education given his continued stagnation in life and the worsening political crisis.

Historical and General Background

In analyzing a historical fiction book, especially one making commentary on relatively modern political events, it is necessary to take a look, even if brief, at the historical background of the events which take place and the background of the author as well. On this basis an explanation of the background of the region and author are provided here.

The known history of east Africa broadly is very long, the longest in the world depending on how you measure it. In fact it likely has one of if not the longest history of homo-sapien settlement of anywhere in the world.[2]

Of relevance to the novel is the various tribes which inhabit Kenya and their lingua franca. The Kikuyu, the tribe of the protagonist, are a Bantu tribe. The Bantu people, as a whole, originated in the hills of what is now eastern Nigeria and western Cameroon. Following the “Bantu expansion” which occurred 3~4,000 years ago, the Bantu peoples came to inhabit much of central, southern, and of note eastern Africa.[3]

Along the east African coast there is a long history of sea trade with peoples outside the continent which had occured for at least 2000 years up until the colonial period. [4] Along this coast was birthed the Swahili people and language which became the lingua franca of much of east Africa. Being the 22nd most spoken language by total speakers globally and the most spoken native African language.

Swahili only really became an important and widely spoken language in the interior of the continent immediately before and during the (European) colonial period with the rise of the Zanzibar sultanate and the slave raids into the interior of the continent and the spread of Islam.[5] This causing the spread of the language as deep as the Congo basin. Which is incredibly far from the coast, though there is immense diversity in the language.

These facts relate to the novel as Swahili is the language spoken by the African characters despite them being Kikuyu and the Kikuyu having their own language. Additionally the tribal traditions and religious beliefs relate to the Kikuyu people, them being a Bantu ethnic group. Many of the differing ethnic groups of Africa had their own indigenous religions which were suppressed upon European colonization but found ways of enduring in folklore in some form or another. The migration of Bantu peoples into east Africa is important to take note of since this means they had roots in the land for over a thousand years before any colonialists arrived to kick them out and from this the reverence of land becomes illuminated.

One of ideological justifications for the struggle for national independence put forth by the Africans in the story is that god, the christian god but also in some regards the traditional Kikuyu god, had bestowed them the land of Kenya, and that Africa was for Africans and Europeans had Europe.

“All the land belonged to the people—black people. They had been given it by God. For every race had its country. The Indians had India. Europeans had Europe. And Africans had Africa, the land of the black people. (Applause) Who did not know that all the soil in this part of the country had been given to Gikuyu and Mumbi and their posterity? (More applause)” (Thiongo, 88)[1]

Then in regards to the mau mau, or the Kenya Land and Freedom Army, they were a Kenyan nationalist group which engaged in armed struggle for the national liberation of Kenya. Though they ultimately failed, in part to successful repression and counter attacks by the British and their alienation of segments of the Kenyan populace who were otherwise in favor of the struggle for national liberation even if it meant armed struggle.[6] Unfortunately resulting in their defeat in 1956, though remnants of the rebellion continued on till independence.

The struggle for land, as evident in the name Kenya Land and Freedom Army, played a key role in the Kenyan struggle. For oppressed nations generally share the common characteristic of the struggle for land and land ownership which has been, by some mechanism or another, denied to them. Dispossession of land from native Africans to make way for British settlers was commonplace in colonial Kenya given that it was a settler colony. Thus the land struggle was particularly acute which gave fertile ground for the ignition of armed conflict and deep resentment broadly to the occupying British.[7]

Lastly, as for the author, Ngugi wa Thiongo, he was born January 5, 1938 and interestingly only passed recently on May 28, 2025. His life and upbringing are similar in certain regards to the events which transpire within the book. Notably the devastation which the mau mau rebellion had on his later childhood and education, with his home village being razed to the ground by the British occupation forces. For higher education, aside from Kenya, he studied in Uganda, the United Kingdom, and the United States.[8] What he is most known for, however, is his writings on decolonization, African language, and things related to those topics. He was a leading African novelist and was significant in the overall development of African literature after direct colonial occupation on the continent ended [9]

In regards to ideology, according to his Wikipedia page he was a “Fanonist Marxist” after his writing of a novel in 1967. In short this means he had a strong tilt toward viewing things via a racial and national lens alongside some influence from the pan-Africanist ideological tradition. This being evident in his writing in this novel, despite it being published in 1964, as the topics revolve mainly around the question of African independence from the perspective of the average African person living at the time. Though the departure here is the general hesitancy toward the armed resistance expressed throughout the novel which is juxtaposed by his later more committed position of support of all armed struggles against colonialism.[10]

Upon these basises the events which transpire within the novel unfold. Particularly the development of the mau mau rebellion and the galvanizing affect this had on Kenyan society at large and the destabilizing role it had on Njoroge’s academic development, family life, and eventual transformation of his thinking on the importance of education.

Key Themes and Message of The Book

One of the primary messages in the novel was that the path to national independence and liberation is complicated and painful. That this process, of the struggle for national independence and liberation, leaves its unquestioning mark on people, crushing their starry eyed dreams into dust under the weight of violence. This affects Njoroge’s view of education by the end of the novel where he is left with nothing in spite of his original view of education as a way out of poverty and a means to help develop the nation.

“He too had once been like this when he had seen the world as a place where a man with learning would rise to power and glory. Then he would never have thought that he would even work for an Indian.” (Thiongo, 175)[1]

Here at the end of the novel Njoroge is left powerless with his long struggle to develop academically ultimately resulting in not much and him reaching such a low point as to be fired from his job while his family is in need of money. He originally thought that education would be a way out of poverty but in the end it is communicated that education alone is insufficient to resolve the problems of society and escape poverty.

Aside from the pursuit of education, the struggle for national independence as being just and necessary is another important theme within the book.

“"But Father says that Kimathi is the leader of the Freedom Army and Jomo is the leader of K.A.U. I like K.A.U. and fear Mau Mau.""But they are all the same? Fighting for the freedom of the black people." This was said by a tall but weak boy. Then with a distant look in his eyes, "I would like to fight in the forest." All eyes were turned to him. He seemed to have said a very profound thing. Or seemed to have put in words what most of them felt.” (Thiongo, 108)[1]

As shown in this excerpt there are competing interpretations of what that would look like exactly. Whether the armed struggle carried out by the mau mau is correct or whether there could be some sort of reconciliation between the native Africans and the British. In spite of the positive portrayal of the act of fighting in the woods for independence as the novel progresses and the mau mau rebellion develops there sharply becomes a shift toward a view of the rebellion as something violent and undesirable. Even if it is in the name of independence from the British colonizers. Which in the end is the concluding tone of the book.

“I have know you for all those years when I was young and foolish and thought of what I could do for my family, my village, and the country. I — now lost all my education, my faith and my It's only now that I do realize how much you had meant to me and how you took an interest in my have family. progress. Because of this it makes all the more painful what my people have done to you. I, alone, am left.” (Thiongo, 179)[1]

Contrasted with the relatively positive outlook on the rebels presented earlier in the novel, towards the end there is much regret on the armed struggle and the promotion of it. Seeing it as only having brought misery and destruction.

The struggle for national independence is also importantly viewed through the lens of land and the people’s connection to land given the displacement and land seizures of the British colonialists. The importance of land, and who owns it, plays a central role in the thinking of the characters in the story as well as the events which transpire in the process of the mau mau rebellion and its prelude. There is much reference to a mixture between the Christian creation story and the folk creation story of the Kikuyu, the tribe of the protagonists of the novel, and how the Kikuyu are “god’s chosen people” and how “this land is theirs not the white man’s”.

“And yet he felt the loss more keenly than Boro, for to him it was a spiritual loss. When a man was severed from the land of his ancestors where would he sacrifice to the Creator? How could he come into contact with the founders of the tribe, Gikuyu and Mumbi? What did of the land even Boro know of oaths, of ancient rites, of the spirits of the ancestors? Still the estrangement cut deeper and deeper into Ngotho's life, emaciating him daily.” (Thiongo, 110)[1]

“His belief in a future for his family and the village rested then not only on a hope for sound education but also on a belief in a God of love and mercy, who long ago walked on this earth with Gikuyu and Mumbi, or Adam and Eve. It did not make much difference that he had come to identify Gikuyu with Adam and Mumbi with Eve. To this God, all men and women were united by one strong bond of brotherhood. And with all this, there was growing up in his heart a feeling that the Gikuyu people, whose land had been taken by white men, were no other than the children of Israel about whom he read in the Bible. So although all men were brothers, the black people had a special mission to the world because they were the chosen people of God.” (Thiongo, 78)[1]

“All the land belonged to the people—black people. They had been given it by God. For every race had its country. The Indians had India. Europeans had Europe. And Africans had Africa, the land of the black people. (Applause) Who did not know that all the soil in this part of the country had been given to Gikuyu and Mumbi and their posterity? (More applause)”[1]

Thus the native African’s connection to the land politically, and in a certain sense spiritually, drives, in no small part, their struggle for national independence. The land is what feeds them in the end, it is their livelihood and so logically the oppressed rural people want to take back the land confiscated from them so many years ago, which the novel frames overall as being just and righteous to certain degree.

And the overall uniting thread of all the themes and emergent core message of the novel is that the struggle for national independence and freedom from colonialism is justified. However the path toward national independence is painful due in large part to the destructive character colonialism has on the colonized society. Even if the colonized are unified in the struggle for independence, colonialism seeks to keep them divided which ultimately leads to social ruin.

“MR. HOWLANDS felt a certain gratifying pleasure. The machine he had set in motion was working. The blacks were destroying the blacks. They would destroy themselves to the end. What did it matter with him if the blacks in the forest destroyed a whole village? What indeed did it matter except for the fact that labor would diminish? Let them destroy themselves. Let them fight against each other. The few who remained would be satisfied with the land the white man had preserved for them. Yes, Mr. Howlands was coming to enjoy his work.” (Thiongo, 139)[1]

Is The Book Good Generally?

Of course I can’t provide an analysis of this book without giving my personal thoughts on the style of the book and whether or not I liked it, and by extension whether I think you should read it as well.

I would generally say that the book was very good, insightful in fact. Despite being east African myself, being part of the diaspora and having been raised abroad completely westernized, I am relatively out of sync with my ethnic roots, though I am not Kikuyu myself there are some similarities among the peoples of the region. I am also not too knowledgeable about Kenyans and their history, despite Kenya being the twin of Tanzania. So the novel was very useful in learning about specifically Kikuyu customs and history, but Kenya as a whole as well. Particularly on what people were thinking of the political goings on at that turbulent time of east African history when we were regaining independence. It is an incredibly valuable insight into the thinking of the people living through the moment at the time and the Kenyan liberation struggle and the forging of their national identity. [11]

I will say I have some disagreements with those who uphold Thiongo and his works, and Thiongo’s positions directly. Namely the over focus on race and nationality and individualist ideas such as “decolonizing the mind” cropping out of post-colonial studies and adjacent fields. That being an over emphasis on the personal struggle in transforming the world and placing ideas above material reality. Rather than accurately recognizing that true liberation and decolonization can only come about via collective struggle in the material concrete realm and not in the realm of ideas first. The mind was “colonized” after all by social factors from without oneself and so can’t be “decolonized” by individual effort. Or even collective efforts while society continually reproduces colonial thinking. And so proper “decolonization of the mind” can’t fully come about till the whole of society is liberated. But even from then it will take a long time to “decolonize” thinking.[12]

Some Lingering Thoughts

And so some lingering thoughts for the reader left implied by the novel are:

- Is armed struggle for the liberation of the oppressed useful or does it end up just exacerbating suffering?

- What really is the value of education in national development and independence, is it the most useful thing in the world or are there other important things for a nation to grasp as well?

- How does the loss of land affect the dignity and social cohesion of the native Africans, is its reclamation something political or something more spiritual?

- What does the portrayal of familial relations, and their disintegration, say about how colonialism tears apart not just communities on the broad level but even families on the particular level?
